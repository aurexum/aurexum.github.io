How the Market Really Works (Orders, Brokers, Liquidity)

  • Journey
  • October 8, 2025

Behind every flashing price chart is a bustling marketplace of buyers, sellers, and middlemen.

Introduction

Imagine this scenario: someone presses “Buy” on their trading app. Within a split second, their order is filled. But what really happened in that moment?

Most beginners think trading is just them versus the market, like buying something directly from a store. In reality, it’s more like entering a massive bazaar, full of merchants, brokers, and crowds - where prices shift depending on who’s buying and selling at that exact second.

If we don’t understand how this marketplace works, we may blame “bad luck” for poor outcomes, when factors like order flow, liquidity, and external events (e.g., news or market volatility) often play significant roles. Understanding these mechanics helps clarify what drives trade results.

The Challenge

Many new traders hold false assumptions that cost them money:

“I trade directly against the market.” In reality, trades happen against other traders’ orders, not some abstract market entity.

“My broker sets the price.” Actually, brokers route orders into the market - though some do take the other side of trades.

“There’s always someone to take my trade.” Not true, low liquidity means orders may not get filled, or get filled at much worse prices.

Because they don’t see behind the curtain, beginners often wonder why their trades didn’t execute at the exact price they clicked, overtrade without realizing how spreads and slippage eat into profits, and trust the wrong kind of broker without understanding the risks.

Key Insights

Here’s what actually happens when we click “buy” or “sell” in the market.

Orders are the fundamental building blocks of every market transaction. Market orders say “Fill me now, at the best available price”, like impatient shoppers who want immediate execution. Limit orders say “Fill me only if price reaches X”, like stall owners setting specific prices and waiting for buyers. Understanding this distinction helps us control execution costs.

Brokers are gatekeepers to the market because individual traders can’t walk directly into exchange trading floors. Direct Market Access (DMA) brokers send orders straight into the marketplace for transparent execution. Market Maker or Dealing Desk brokers sometimes take the other side of trades themselves. Knowing the broker type matters significantly. It affects spreads, execution speed, and even potential conflicts of interest.

Liquidity is the lifeblood that determines how smoothly markets function. Liquidity means how easy it is to buy or sell without moving the price significantly. In highly liquid markets like EUR/USD or Apple stock, orders fill instantly at tight spreads. In illiquid markets like penny stocks or exotic pairs, traders face wide spreads, slippage, or partial fills. This is one reason professional traders often prefer major markets, where high liquidity supports tighter spreads and faster execution, compared to illiquid markets like a deserted alley. Other factors, like market depth or regulatory stability, may also influence their choices.

Closing Thought

Markets are not mysterious casinos. They are living marketplaces with clear mechanics. Once we understand orders, brokers, and liquidity, we stop blaming “luck” and start seeing the structure that drives every trade.
